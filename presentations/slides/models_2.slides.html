Predicting next based on current feature values¶

Structure¶

Predictors:

  • Valence of track $i$
  • Energy of track $i$
  • Loudness of track $i$
  • Tempo of track $i$
  • Track number of $i$ (1 to 16)

Dependent variable:

  • Valence/energy/loudness/tempo of track $i+1$

Model 1 - Splines regression¶

Overall, we can explain only a small ammount of variance from our data. All models are significant, and the coefficients of all variables of interest are significant. Only some interactions are significant. I didn't look deep for effects of different variables because of the very low R2.

Only thing I find interesting here is the fact that tempo has the lowest R2, which is consistent with what we found here (small difference in tempo between positions).

Summary of results¶

So far, we have only looked at the diagnostics from the model fitted in the training data set. We looked at the significance of different predictor variables and the R2 that we got for different dependent variables. Overall conclusions:

  • No consistent interaction effects (splines);
  • Track number is either the most relevant or the second most relevant variable to predict feature of track in position $i+1$.
  • Predictor variables $f$ is either the best or the second best variable to predict the value of $f$ in position $i+1$ (splines and random forest)

Model comparison - test dataset¶

Now I'll do a cross validation of these models and evaluate their performance in terms of RMSE. In case Random Forest is not overfitting, we should see a lower RMSE for it, since its R2 was 10 times higher than the R2 of the splines regression (~0.01 agains ~0.1)

Discussion¶

Track number seems to be the most important feature for predicting next energy and loudness. For predicting next valence and tempo, the current value of these features is the most important. This is consistent with the splines regression, which shows that the current feature is the best predictor of the next feature.

However, this worries me for the following reason:

Problem¶

The question that we're asking here is: "can we predict the feature of track $i+1$ given the feature(s) of track $i$?

If we look at the pre-normalized distribution of say, loudness, we could hypothesize that, given an observed track (blue line), the loudness of the next track would, most likely, be higher (e.g. red line). After z-score transformation, the distribution gets less skewed, and more like a gaussian curve. Still, this problem would remain.

So I'm wondering: given a normalized valence level, a non linear model, such as splines, should be able to predict, to some extent, the valence of the next track, simply due to the probability density function.

My point: If we use the current feature to predict the next feature, we might be asking the wrong question, since values at the extreme of the distribution will most lileky be followed by more probable values (a kind of regression towards the mean effect).

I'm not absolutely sure about what I just said, but perheps you can help me see whether my worry makes sense or not.
